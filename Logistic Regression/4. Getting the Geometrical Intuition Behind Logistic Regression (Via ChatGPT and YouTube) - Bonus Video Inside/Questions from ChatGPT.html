What's the decision boundary of logistic regression based model like?


The decision boundary of a logistic regression model is the threshold at which the predicted probability of the positive class is equal to a certain value, usually 0.5. In a binary classification problem, this boundary separates the feature space into two regions, each corresponding to one of the classes.
For a logistic regression model, the decision boundary is defined by the equation:
β0+β1x1+β2x2+…+βnxn=0\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \ldots + \beta_n x_n = 0β0​+β1​x1​+β2​x2​+…+βn​xn​=0
where β0\beta_0β0​ is the intercept, and β1,β2,…,βn\beta_1, \beta_2, \ldots, \beta_nβ1​,β2​,…,βn​ are the coefficients of the features x1,x2,…,xnx_1, x_2, \ldots, x_nx1​,x2​,…,xn​.
Characteristics of the Decision Boundary
  1. Linear Nature: The decision boundary of logistic regression is a linear combination of the features. This means that in the feature space, it is a hyperplane. For example:
    • In a 2D feature space, the decision boundary is a line.
    • In a 3D feature space, it is a plane.
    • In higher dimensions, it is a hyperplane.
  2. Separating Regions: The boundary divides the feature space into two regions:
    • One where the model predicts the positive class (probability > 0.5).
    • One where the model predicts the negative class (probability < 0.5).
  3. Interpretation: Points on the decision boundary have an equal probability (usually 0.5) of belonging to either class.
Example in 2D Feature Space
For a logistic regression model with two features x1x_1x1​ and x2x_2x2​, the decision boundary can be expressed as:
β0+β1x1+β2x2=0\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 = 0β0​+β1​x1​+β2​x2​=0
Solving for x2x_2x2​, we get:
x2=−β0β2−β1β2x1x_2 = -\frac{\beta_0}{\beta_2} - \frac{\beta_1}{\beta_2} x_1x2​=−β2​β0​​−β2​β1​​x1​
This equation represents a straight line in the 2D feature space.

What does it mean by odds ratio?


The odds ratio (OR) is a measure used in statistics to quantify the strength of the association between two events, typically in the context of logistic regression, case-control studies, or clinical trials. It compares the odds of an event occurring in one group to the odds of it occurring in another group.
Understanding Odds and Odds Ratio
Odds
Odds are a way of expressing the likelihood of an event. They are calculated as:
Odds=P(event)1−P(event)\text{Odds} = \frac{P(\text{event})}{1 - P(\text{event})}Odds=1−P(event)P(event)​
where P(event)P(\text{event})P(event) is the probability of the event occurring.
For example, if the probability of an event is 0.75 (or 75%), the odds are:
Odds=0.751−0.75=0.750.25=3\text{Odds} = \frac{0.75}{1 - 0.75} = \frac{0.75}{0.25} = 3Odds=1−0.750.75​=0.250.75​=3
This means the event is three times as likely to occur as not to occur.
Odds Ratio
The odds ratio is the ratio of the odds of an event occurring in one group to the odds of it occurring in another group. It is calculated as:
Odds Ratio=Odds in Group 1Odds in Group 2\text{Odds Ratio} = \frac{\text{Odds in Group 1}}{\text{Odds in Group 2}}Odds Ratio=Odds in Group 2Odds in Group 1​
Interpretation
  • OR = 1: The event is equally likely in both groups.
  • OR > 1: The event is more likely in Group 1 than in Group 2.
  • OR < 1: The event is less likely in Group 1 than in Group 2.
Example in Logistic Regression
In the context of logistic regression, the odds ratio helps interpret the effect of a predictor variable. Suppose we have a logistic regression model:
logit(P)=β0+β1x1\text{logit}(P) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1logit(P)=β0​+β1​x1​
The odds ratio for a one-unit increase in x1x_1x1​ is given by eβ1e^{\beta_1}eβ1​.
  • If β1>0\beta_1 > 0β1​>0, eβ1e^{\beta_1}eβ1​ will be greater than 1, indicating that as x1x_1x1​ increases, the odds of the event increase.
  • If β1<0\beta_1 < 0β1​<0, eβ1e^{\beta_1}eβ1​ will be less than 1, indicating that as x1x_1x1​ increases, the odds of the event decrease.
Practical Example
Consider a study on the effect of a new drug on a certain medical condition. The odds ratio might be used to compare the odds of improvement in the treatment group to the odds of improvement in the control group.
  • Odds in the treatment group: Number of improvements / Number of no improvements
  • Odds in the control group: Number of improvements / Number of no improvements
If the odds ratio is 2, it means that the odds of improvement are twice as high in the treatment group compared to the control group.
Summary
The odds ratio is a valuable measure for understanding the relationship between two variables, especially in logistic regression and other statistical analyses. It provides insight into how the odds of an event change with respect to different conditions or treatments.

What is the intuition behind this kind of decision boundary from a logistic regression model?


The intuition behind the decision boundary of a logistic regression model lies in how it models the probability of an outcome based on the input features. Let's break down the intuition step-by-step:
Logistic Regression Basics
  1. Linear Relationship: Logistic regression models the relationship between input features and the log-odds of the binary outcome as a linear combination. Mathematically, for a binary classification with features x1,x2,…,xnx_1, x_2, \ldots, x_nx1​,x2​,…,xn​, the linear model is:
    z=β0+β1x1+β2x2+…+βnxnz = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \ldots + \beta_n x_nz=β0​+β1​x1​+β2​x2​+…+βn​xn​
  2. Logistic Function: The logistic (or sigmoid) function transforms this linear combination into a probability:
    P(y=1∣x)=σ(z)=11+e−zP(y=1|x) = \sigma(z) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z}}P(y=1∣x)=σ(z)=1+e−z1​
    This transformation maps any real-valued number into the range (0, 1), representing the probability of the positive class.
Decision Boundary
  1. Probability Threshold: The decision boundary is determined by a probability threshold, often 0.5. This means the boundary is where the model is indifferent between the classes, i.e., where the probability is exactly 0.5.
  2. Equation of the Boundary: Setting P(y=1∣x)=0.5P(y=1|x) = 0.5P(y=1∣x)=0.5, we find:
    σ(z)=0.5⇒z=0\sigma(z) = 0.5 \quad \Rightarrow \quad z = 0σ(z)=0.5⇒z=0
    Therefore, the decision boundary is defined by:
    β0+β1x1+β2x2+…+βnxn=0\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \ldots + \beta_n x_n = 0β0​+β1​x1​+β2​x2​+…+βn​xn​=0
    This equation describes a hyperplane in the feature space.
  3. Interpretation: The decision boundary separates the feature space into regions corresponding to different predicted classes:
    • On one side of the boundary, the predicted probability is greater than 0.5, indicating the positive class.
    • On the other side, the probability is less than 0.5, indicating the negative class.
Intuition Behind the Decision Boundary
  1. Linear Separation: Since logistic regression is a linear model, it assumes that the classes can be separated by a linear boundary. This is ideal for linearly separable data but may not perform well if the true boundary is non-linear.
  2. Influence of Features: Each feature contributes to the decision boundary according to its coefficient. A positive coefficient means the feature increases the probability of the positive class, while a negative coefficient decreases it.
  3. Weight and Importance: The magnitude of the coefficients indicates the strength of each feature's contribution to the decision boundary. Larger coefficients mean the feature has a more significant impact on the prediction.

  • Points above the line (if β1\beta_1β1​ and β2\beta_2β2​ are positive) are more likely to be in the positive class, while those below are more likely to be in the negative class.
Summary
The decision boundary of a logistic regression model reflects the balance point where the model is equally likely to predict either class. It is a result of the linear relationship modeled between the features and the log-odds of the outcome, and it helps classify observations by determining which side of the boundary they fall on based on their feature values.
